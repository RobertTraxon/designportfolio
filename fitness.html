Problem

I wanted to design a product that would tackle the issues that lead so many to fail when using fitness apps with exercise.

Research

Most people have used a fitness app at some stage in their life, but few maintain the commitment they often take. I wanted to get a better understanding of what drives people to use them, and the general opinions on their effectiveness. Here were some of the key questions I considered when gathering data for this project:

  1. Who uses fitness trackers?
  2. What are peoples main pain-points?
  3. What features are most important to users?
  4. What are the best methods for user-recursion?

Time commitments are a common complaint among many existing fitness apps.
A study looking at fitness app usage patterns shows a significant decrease over time and a general decline in perception of usefulness and ease.
Gamification in fitness apps can be problematic and often lead to a hit-or-quit attitude.
Viewing progress and setting goals are still the most crucial features users want to see.
Fitness apps are generally used by a wide variety of different age groups and experience levels and are often used by exercise professionals to help motivate and track clients.

Solution

Design a fitness app that provides users with a healthier approach to exercise and motivates without feeling like a burden.

Neutral Tone

The main issue that needs addressing with the creation of a new fitness app is the perceived perception of the product itself. A focus on adopting a more neutral tone when trying to encourage users and providing a personalised experience will be key to avoiding the negative perceptions that force users to delete fitness apps altogether.
Customisation

Most apps that exist tend to cater better to a specific goal than others. Many put too much emphasis on calorie counting or cardio trackers, despite fitness goals often being the opposite. The intention for this product is to provide a platform that can be tailored to each individual's needs and goals.

Prototype
Familiarity

The studies show that the perceived ease of exercise apps generally decreased over time, starting from an already low level. The most effective way to combat this would be to structure the app in such a way that the user can draw familiarity with other products or services they use regularly. As a high percentage of users will have experience with apps like Google Calendar, I decided a calendar-style format would be a great way to give users confidence when using the app.

Customisation

Experience levels and motivations with trackers can vary dramatically. This is why I wanted to create an experience that is tailored to the user's goals and allows a component-style dashboard for people to choose the type of information that is presented to them. The ability to hide or show features like calorie counters, volume metrics, and productivity statistics will help to create a more fluid platform and avoid feelings of failure for users.

Programming

With so many users downloading fitness apps to receive information and guidance with their workouts, I also wanted to create a section that could provide this. Adding a professionally reviewed database of exercises, workouts, and fitness programmes would allow users a quick and effective way to achieve their goals under the guidance of professionals.Â 

Save Time

With the app being very feature-heavy, it was important to implement additional time-saving abilities that would make the process feel less involved for the user. Having recommended exercises based on previous use or predictive behaviours would not only save the user time but also help less experienced customers build an effective programme.

Professional Oversight

Another ability I wanted to add was data sharing with approved users for athletes that have a coach or personal trainer. This would allow the coach to directly programme exercises into their client's calendar and receive data on their performance, saving valuable time for both parties.
