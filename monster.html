Problem

We wanted to fill the content gap for the EYFS market by designing a product that could help young children with the 7 key areas of learning.

Research

For this project, we had the opportunity to gather in-person research from children at the early years level as well as data from teachers and parents. Here were some of the key questions we considered when gathering information for this project:

  1. What are the key areas of learning at the EYFS level?
  2. What existing products are already out there?
  3. What are the limitations of educational games at the EYFS level?
  4. How are children using educational games?

We discussed the seven key areas of learning with the EYFS team to ensure we could create a product to cover all aspects needed at that age.
Many of the existing products available brought together multiple mini-games into a package that covered the main areas separately.
Early years learners struggle with mouse movement, so a higher emphasis on touch enabled devices should be considered.
Characters and well-defined themes are often important to create a sense of familiarity when reinforcing learning.
Bigger trigger areas for buttons and better placement for key functionality should be considered.
User Needs
Engaging
Shapes, colours, and sounds play an important role in creating engaging content for children under 5.
Simple
Whether it is the user experience or the user interface itself, simplicity is key for EYFS.
Explorative
Rather than it feeling like a task, it's important that games at this age are explorative and not punish users for making decisions.
Business Needs
New Market
This package should help drive traffic from a relatively untouched market towards our game platform.
Educational
Digital device usage is a controversial subject at this age, so it's important to provide a purposeful product led by education.
Solution

Create a package of games covering the 7 key areas of learning and re-design the UI system to be mores suitable for EYFS users.

Scene Builder

With a total of 9 mini-games covering the 7 key areas, we split the load between the team, and I was tasked with creating 2 games covering expressive arts and design. This first one would be a visual scene builder that would allow users to create a custom image using a variety of different movable assets.

Musical Monsters

In the second game, I wanted to focus on audio, rhythm, and music to stimulate different senses than the first game. Originally, I wanted this to be a scaled-down audio interface, but after gathering a better understanding of ability levels at EYFS, I decided to use pre-recorded loops that could be interchanged and layered to create different combinations of music.

Prototype
Monster Island

As a group, we decided pretty early on to combine the games into an island, so users could interact with a map and decide which mini-games they wanted to explore. As characters proved to be extremely valuable in the research we did, a selection of monsters were illustrated that could be used throughout this game pack and in future resources.

Scene Builder

One of the other key areas of learning (understanding the world) didn't have much coverage throughout the other mini-games, so I decided the content of the scene builder could integrate with this. Having different environments, clothing for the monsters, and weather-specific objects would allow users to develop their understanding of the world.

Musical Monsters

I thought the musical monster game would be a great way to bring together all the characters, where the user could select which monsters they wanted to form a band on stage. Each monster would have an individual instrument loop assigned, which would all work together to form different melodies and rhythms. This would be a simple yet effective way for children to express their creativity through music.
